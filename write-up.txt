Trader Performance vs Market Sentiment: Project Write-up

This document summarizes the approach, key findings, and actionable trading rules discovered by analyzing the relationship between the Bitcoin Fear & Greed Index and trader performance on Hyperliquid.

1. Methodology

The goal was to identify distinct behavioral shifts in trader performance driven by overarching market sentiment.
Data Alignment: Raw Hyperliquid execution data (containing unix timestamps, sizing, and order direction) was normalized into daily UTC boundaries to align perfectly with the daily Fear/Greed classifications.
Metric Aggregation: For each trader (Account) on an active day, we calculated the total Daily PnL, Win Rate (profitable closed trades / total closed trades), average Trade Size (Volume USD), trade Frequency, and a Long/Short Ratio.
Trader Segmentation: Traders were clustered into generalized segments to prevent outlier bias. We used global medians to designate traders as either Frequent vs Infrequent and High-Volume vs Low-Volume.
Analysis: The final merged data was grouped by sentiment regimes (Fear, Greed, Neutral) allowing us to observe how metrics and segment-level PnLs directly skewed amidst shifting market psychology.

2. Key Insights

Our statistical analysis uncovered clear behavioral shifts on "Fear" days:

* Insight 1: Panic "Dip-Buying" Leads to Oversizing
During Fear regimes, the average trader actively increases their trade count (47.3 trades/day vs 41.2 on Greed) whilst simultaneously bloating their average trade size ($8,529 vs $5,954). Furthermore, the Long/Short ratio spikes strictly toward longing (8.37 vs 5.72). Traders predictably attempt to aggressively catch falling knives during market drawdowns.

* Insight 2: "Chop" Destroys the Overtrader
Under the 'Fear' classification, High-Volume traders who trade Frequently significantly underperform (Avg PnL: $3,894) compared to High-Volume traders who trade Infrequently (Avg PnL: $17,834). High volatility on Fear days leads to massive chop. Taking a single, well-calculated position performs roughly 4.5x better than attempting to scalp volatile panic.

* Insight 3: The Retail Disadvantage in Fear
The "Infrequent + Low Volume" (Retail) trader performs awfully on Fear days (65.5% win rate, $811 daily PnL). Yet, during Greed regimes, this exact same subset achieves an outstanding 93.7% win rate. Lower volume accounts lack the liquid margin needed to survive extreme drawdown wicks under turbulence, causing wide systemic capitulation.

3. Strategy Recommendations

Based on the quantitative segmentation, we recommend the following two actionable trading strategies:

Strategy A: VIX/Fear Volume Throttling (For High-Volume/Algorithmic Accounts)
* The Rule: When Sentiment enters 'Fear' or 'Extreme Fear', programmatically reduce trade velocity (execution frequency per hour) by 50%, but allow position sizing to remain at normal levels for concentrated entries.
* Rationale: Escaping the high-volatility "chop zone" prevents death-by-a-thousand-cuts. The data heavily supports infrequent, high-volume action dominating the fear chop.

Strategy B: Long-Only Trend Safety (For Retail / Low-Volume Accounts)
* The Rule: If operating a Low-Volume strategy, implement a strict halt on deploying capital during Fear days. Only execute setups when the sentiment transitions solidly back into 'Greed' or 'Neutral'.
* Rationale: Moving from a ~65% win-rate knife-catching environment to a mathematically backed ~93% structural momentum scenario guarantees systemic profitability over an extended horizon. Retail traders should strictly avoid fighting extreme systemic fear.
